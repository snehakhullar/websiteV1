About

I'm a User Experience professional who is passionate about creating simple and delightful products.

UX designer

Researcher

Designer

Architect

During the past two years at Esri, I’ve worked very closely with maps and business intelligence products. I strive to make complex analytics tools easy to use for non-technical users. I play roles of – researcher, visual designer, information architect, and usability analyst across various products.

I've extended my role from a UX designer to a UX coach, by leading design workshops for developers and products engineers. I believe that to create delightful product, UxD philosophy needs to be adapted across the company.

In my grad school, I worked on a range of design research projects which I prototyped using Processing, Arduino, WebGL and D3 library. I took courses across several schools (still vividly remember biking across campuses, in bone-chilling Boston winter) such as Data visualization  in Harvard Engineering School, Product Design  in MIT Media Lab and Managing Innovations  in Harvard Business School.

I worked as a design researcher at metaLAB, Harvard University and at the Mobile experience lab, MIT where I contributed to developing cutting edge solutions for digital libraries and note-taking, respectively.

I was invited by a professor to establish a design start-up, a week after finishing my undergrad architecture thesis. This venture exposed me to several different domains where design thinking could be applied.

Through various multi-disciplinary projects, I developed a passion for user-centred design, which led me to purse a graduate degree in this field at Harvard University, and later to choose User Experience as my profession.

I believe that my architecture education has given me a strong design foundation. A project that shaped my design philosophy is the construction of a primary school  in an incredibly remote Indian village, called Kargyak.

Kargyak is situated in the Indian Himalayas, at an altitude of 4200m, and is a 5 day hike from the nearest road access point. The location constraints offered numerous unexpected challenges that made me a resilient designer – fail fast and recover strong.
